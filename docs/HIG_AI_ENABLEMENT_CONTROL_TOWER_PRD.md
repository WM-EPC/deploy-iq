## 1. Executive Summary

The current Deploy IQ application is an OpenAI-focused enterprise AI deployment toolkit. It is a strong baseline, but the H.I.G. Capital role requires a broader operating system: a multi-platform, portfolio-scale enablement control tower that coordinates AI training, adoption, governance, vendor content, champions, compliance, and ROI across H.I.G. and its portfolio companies.

This PRD defines the pivot from "OpenAI deployment toolkit" to "H.I.G. AI Enablement Control Tower."

The new product should demonstrate the central thesis for the Director of AI Training & Enablement role:

> H.I.G. should not manage AI training as scattered workshops. It should run AI enablement as a portfolio operations system, with segmentation, repeatable curricula, measurable adoption, vendor governance, and leadership-ready ROI reporting.

The app should make Bill's approach visible in five minutes:

- He understands private equity portfolio operations.
- He can train across frontier AI platforms, not only OpenAI.
- He can coordinate many stakeholders across offices, vendors, and portfolio companies.
- He can connect training activity to business adoption and ROI.
- He can build the tracking mechanism needed to operate this function at scale.

## 2. Role Interpretation

### What H.I.G. Is Hiring For

The role is not a pure learning and development position. It sits in Portfolio Operations and combines:

- Curriculum strategy
- Training operations
- Vendor management
- Platform fluency
- Compliance reporting
- Direct facilitation
- Change management
- Adoption analytics
- ROI measurement
- Portfolio company stakeholder management

The director must support two audiences at once:

- **Firm-side:** H.I.G. investment professionals, operating partners, regional offices, deal teams, and internal AI champions.
- **Portfolio-side:** executives and operators across 100+ portfolio companies with varied industries, maturities, tools, and business priorities.

### Core Job-To-Be-Done

Create a repeatable system that helps H.I.G. and its portfolio companies:

1. Know which AI tools are approved and best suited for which workflows.
2. Train the right audiences at the right level.
3. Coordinate vendors and content refreshes as platforms evolve.
4. Track completion, confidence, adoption, and compliance.
5. Convert training into measurable business outcomes.
6. Identify where the portfolio needs intervention.

### Interview Positioning

The MVP should let Bill say:

> "I would run this like a portfolio value creation program, not a course catalog. The control tower gives H.I.G. one operating view across companies, platforms, training activity, champions, risk, adoption, and ROI."

## 3. Company Context

### H.I.G. Capital Context

H.I.G. Capital is a global private equity and alternative investment firm focused on the middle market. Public materials describe H.I.G. as operating across private equity, growth equity, real estate, direct lending, special situation credit, and healthcare growth. The firm emphasizes hands-on operational, consulting, technology, and financial management experience to help portfolio companies create value.

This has direct product implications:

- The app must feel like an operating platform, not an educational microsite.
- Portfolio segmentation matters because middle-market companies vary widely in AI maturity.
- ROI must be expressed in operating metrics: margin expansion, productivity, cycle time, working capital, customer support efficiency, finance automation, diligence speed, and management visibility.
- Training must be tied to the PE value creation agenda, not abstract AI literacy.

### Portfolio Company Context

H.I.G.'s public portfolio spans industries such as business services, healthcare, industrials, consumer, technology, and financial services. The control tower should be able to represent companies with different needs:

- A healthcare services company with HIPAA concerns.
- A software or digital services company with developer productivity use cases.
- A manufacturing or industrial company focused on supply chain, finance, and operations.
- A marketing services company focused on content, analytics, and campaign operations.
- A portfolio company with low maturity that needs safe AI literacy before advanced tooling.

### AI Stack Context

The job description names H.I.G.'s AI technology stack as including:

- Anthropic Claude / Claude Enterprise
- OpenAI ChatGPT Team / Enterprise
- ToltIQ
- Emerging platforms

The app should also account for other frontier and enterprise platforms likely to appear in training conversations:

- Google Gemini
- Microsoft Copilot
- Perplexity Enterprise or research-oriented tools
- Internal GPTs / custom assistants
- API-based AI systems and agents

Important phrasing:

- ToltIQ should be described as an H.I.G.-approved AI due diligence platform or firm-wide diligence platform, not as "internal AI" unless H.I.G. uses that language directly.
- The app should train on "approved AI tools and usage patterns" rather than implying every platform is available to every audience.

## 4. Product Vision

### Vision Statement

H.I.G. AI Enablement Control Tower is a portfolio-scale operating system for AI upskilling, adoption, governance, and ROI measurement across H.I.G. and its portfolio companies.

### Product Promise

The control tower answers four executive questions:

1. **Where are we?** AI readiness, training completion, adoption, and risk by office, company, sector, and role.
2. **What is happening next?** Training calendar, vendor deliverables, office hours, cohort launches, and required compliance events.
3. **Where are we stuck?** Companies or teams with low adoption, missing sponsors, content gaps, expired training, or unresolved governance issues.
4. **What value are we creating?** Usage growth, workflow adoption, time savings, productivity gains, cycle-time reductions, and ROI narratives.

### Product Principles

- **Portfolio-first:** Design around many companies, not one enterprise customer.
- **Multi-platform:** Train across frontier platforms and H.I.G.-approved tools.
- **Outcome-backed:** Every training path maps to business use cases and KPIs.
- **Operationally realistic:** Include vendors, calendars, compliance, champions, and refresh cycles.
- **Executive-readable:** Leadership should understand the dashboard in under one minute.
- **Facilitator-useful:** The director should be able to run the job from the app.

## 6. Operating Model

### Hub-And-Spoke Structure

The control tower supports a hub-and-spoke enablement model:

- **Hub:** H.I.G. AI Enablement Office, owned by the Director.
- **Regional spokes:** H.I.G. offices and operating partners.
- **Portfolio spokes:** AI champions and executive sponsors inside portfolio companies.
- **Vendor layer:** External training partners creating and maintaining content.
- **Governance layer:** approved-use policy, risk escalation, compliance reporting, data handling guidance.

### Portfolio Segmentation

The MVP should segment portfolio companies into enablement tiers.

**Tier 1: Lighthouse Companies**

- High readiness, strong executive sponsor, clear use cases.
- Receive deeper intervention and ROI tracking.
- Goal: create internal case studies and reusable playbooks.

**Tier 2: Cohort Companies**

- Moderate readiness, shared functional needs.
- Grouped by sector, function, or maturity.
- Goal: scalable learning paths and repeatable workshops.

**Tier 3: Foundation Companies**

- Early maturity or limited bandwidth.
- Receive baseline AI literacy, governance, office hours, and self-serve resources.
- Goal: safe adoption and readiness improvement.

### Training Lifecycle

The control tower tracks the end-to-end lifecycle:

1. Assess maturity and needs.
2. Assign segment and learning paths.
3. Confirm sponsor and champions.
4. Schedule required sessions.
5. Coordinate vendor deliverables.
6. Deliver training and labs.
7. Track completion and knowledge checks.
8. Monitor adoption and workflow usage.
9. Quantify ROI.
10. Refresh content as platforms change.

## 7. Platform Coverage

The product should include a platform coverage matrix that maps each AI platform to its best-fit audiences, workflows, risks, and training modules.

| Platform | Primary H.I.G. Relevance | Training Focus | Example Use Cases | Governance Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ToltIQ | Due diligence and private markets workflows | VDR querying, source-linked analysis, prompt library, diligence QA | CIM analysis, risk extraction, customer concentration, contract review, market diligence | Sensitive deal data, source verification, access controls |
| ChatGPT Team / Enterprise | General productivity and custom assistants | prompt patterns, data analysis, GPTs, projects, enterprise controls | memo drafting, research synthesis, meeting prep, finance analysis, operations support | data boundaries, approved connectors, sensitive info handling |
| Claude Enterprise | Long-context reasoning and document-heavy workflows | document analysis, artifacts, structured writing, comparison tasks | policy review, board memo drafting, research synthesis, contract summarization | prompt hygiene, source validation, confidentiality |
| Google Gemini | Workspace-native productivity and multimodal workflows | Docs, Sheets, Gmail, Drive, meetings, multimodal review | email drafting, spreadsheet help, meeting summaries, deck support | workspace permissions and data access |
| Microsoft Copilot | Microsoft 365 productivity | Outlook, Teams, Excel, PowerPoint, Word workflows | meeting recap, Excel analysis, deck generation, document drafting | tenant governance, permissions inheritance |
| API / Agents | Advanced automation and productized AI | evals, agents, RAG, workflow automation, monitoring | customer support automation, internal tools, finance workflows | evals, auditability, human escalation |
| Emerging Tools | Rapidly changing platform landscape | tool evaluation and safe experimentation | research, image/video, workflow automation | vendor review, data policy, refresh cadence |

The MVP does not need live integrations. It needs to show the director has a structured way to manage training across these platforms.

## 9. Core Workflows

### Workflow 1: Weekly Control Tower Review

The Director opens the control tower and answers:

- Which companies are green, yellow, red?
- Which upcoming trainings need attention?
- Which vendors owe deliverables?
- Which companies have compliance gaps?
- Which companies have strong adoption but weak ROI capture?
- What should leadership know this week?

Output:

- Prioritized next-action queue.
- 3-5 leadership talking points.
- Intervention list for operating partners.

### Workflow 2: Portfolio Company Onboarding

The Director or operating partner adds a portfolio company to the enablement program.

Inputs:

- Sector
- Region
- Employee count
- AI maturity
- Existing tools
- Executive sponsor
- Champion candidates
- Compliance requirements
- Priority functions
- Business outcomes

Output:

- Segment assignment.
- Recommended curriculum.
- Required training calendar.
- Champion plan.
- First 90-day roadmap.

### Workflow 3: Training Program Management

The Director plans a training cohort across companies.

Inputs:

- Audience
- Platform
- Skill level
- Vendor or internal delivery
- Required completion date
- Follow-up office hours
- Assessment method

Output:

- Calendar entry.
- Participant list.
- Required pre-work.
- Post-training survey and knowledge check.
- Adoption tracking plan.

### Workflow 4: Vendor Content Refresh

The Director sees a platform release or capability change and updates training materials.

Inputs:

- Platform affected
- Release type
- Audience impact
- Existing content modules
- Vendor owner
- Review deadline

Output:

- Content refresh task.
- Vendor brief.
- Quality review checkpoint.
- Approval status.
- Change log.

### Workflow 5: ROI Review

The Director prepares leadership reporting.

Inputs:

- Training completion
- Adoption metrics
- Workflow use cases
- Estimated time savings
- Business KPI movement
- Qualitative feedback

Output:

- Executive summary.
- ROI scorecard.
- Case study candidates.
- Investment recommendations.

## 14. Measurement Framework

### Training Effectiveness

Track more than attendance.

- Completion rate.
- Satisfaction.
- Knowledge retention.
- Hands-on lab performance.
- Confidence lift.
- Time-to-first-use after training.

### Adoption

- Active users by platform.
- Frequency of use.
- Use case submissions.
- Prompt/workflow reuse.
- Custom assistants or approved workflows created.
- Office hours participation.
- Champion coaching activity.

### Governance

- Mandatory training completion.
- Policy acknowledgement.
- High-risk workflow escalation.
- Data handling incidents.
- Content freshness.
- Vendor review completion.

### ROI

- Time saved.
- Cycle time reduced.
- Manual work removed.
- Quality improved.
- Revenue or margin opportunity.
- Risk reduction.
- Productivity gain.

### Reporting Cadence

- Weekly: operational dashboard.
- Monthly: portfolio adoption review.
- Quarterly: executive ROI and governance review.
- After major platform releases: content refresh review.
